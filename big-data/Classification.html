Introductory Example:

A loan company wants to create a set of rules to identify potential defaulters. They have categorical data on 10000 customers concerning: repayement statut (Default or No default. 500 of customers were defaulters); home statut (owner, tenant, other); annual income (<30K, 30-70K, >70K); Age (18-25, 26-40, 41-55, 55+); marital statut (married, divorced, single, widow, other); Criminal Record? (Yes, No); time with employer (<1, 1-5, >5) ; Credit card Owner (Yes, No) (years).

Remark: In the previous example, the attribute repayement statut is the outcome and the others are input variables.

Objectives

Given historical information about customers, we want:

  • To establish a rule whereby we can classify a new customer (on the basis of observed attributes or features) into one of the existing classes (Supervised Learning)

  • Prediction based on observed attributes

Definition

  • Entropy: measures the impurity (or absence of information) of an attribute. Given an attribute \(X\) and its label \(x\), let \(P(x)\) be the probability of \(x\). The entropy of \(X\), \(H_X\), is defined as \[ \begin{aligned} H_X=-\sum_{\forall x \in X}P(x)log_2 (P(x)) \end{aligned} \] where \(log_2(u)=ln(u)/ln(2)\)
  • The base entropy: the entropy of the outcome variable;
  • The conditional entropy: Given an attribute X, its value x, the outcome variable Y and its value y, the conditional entropy \(H_{Y|X}\) is the remaining entropy of Y given X, defined as: \[ \begin{aligned} H_{Y|X}=-\sum_{\forall x \in X}P(x)\sum_{\forall y \in Y} P(y|x)log_2 (P(y|x)) \end{aligned} \]
  • The information gain of an attribute X: the difference between the base entropy \(H_Y\) and the conditional entropy of the attribute \(H_{Y|X}\) \[ \begin{aligned} InfoGain_X=H_Y-H_{Y|X} \end{aligned} \]

Decision trees algorithm: ID3 (Iterative Dichotomiser 3)

Let A be a set of categorical input variables, Y be the output variable (or the predicted class), and T be the training set.

  • Step 1: Compute information gain for each attribute in A relative to T

  • Step 2: Pick the attribute X with the largest information gain. Let \({x_1,x_2,...,x_m}\) be the values of attribute X

  • Partition T into \({T_1,T_2,...,T_m}\) according to the values of X

  • Return a tree with root X and branches labeled \({x_1,x_2,...,x_m}\)

  • Recurse on subsets using remaining attributes: move respectively to trees \(ID3(A-{X}, Y, T_1)\), \(ID3(A-{X}, Y,T_2)\),,\(ID3(A-{X}, Y, T_m)\)

Decision trees with R: a case study

The data is related with direct marketing campaigns of a Portuguese banking institution. The marketing campaigns were based on phone calls. Often, more than one contact to the same client was required, in order to access if the product (bank term deposit) would be (yes) or not (no) subscribed.

  • Attribute Information:

    • Input variables (bank client data): 1-age (numeric); 2-job : type of job (categorical: admin.,blue-collar,entrepreneur,housemaid,management,retired,self-employed,services,student,technician,unemployed,unknown); 3-marital : marital status (categorical: divorced,married,single,unknown; note: divorced means divorced or widowed); 4-education (categorical: primary,secondary,tertiary,unknown); 5-default: has credit in default? (categorical: no,yes,unknown); 6-housing: has housing loan? (categorical: no,yes,unknown); 7-loan: has personal loan? (categorical: no,yes,unknown); 8-contact: contact communication type (categorical: cellular,telephone); 9-month: last contact month of year (categorical: jan, feb, mar, , nov, dec); 10-duration: last contact duration, in seconds (numeric); 11 - campaign: number of contacts performed during this campaign and for this client (numeric, includes last contact); 12-pdays: number of days that passed by after the client was last contacted from a previous campaign (numeric; 999 means client was not previously contacted) 13 - previous: number of contacts performed before this campaign and for this client (numeric); 15-poutcome: outcome of the previous marketing campaign (categorical: failure,nonexistent,success); 16-day_of_week: last contact day of the week (categorical: mon,tue,wed,thu,fri).

    • Output variable (desired target): 17-y: has the client subscribed a term deposit? (binary: yes,no);

Nave Bayes algorithm

It is a classification technique based on Bayes Theorem.

Definition: the Bayestheorem

It provides a way of calculating posterior probability P(C|X) from P(C), P(X) and P(X|C): \[ \begin{aligned} P(C|X)=\frac{P(X|C)P(C)}{P(X)} \end{aligned} \] where

  • P(C|X) is the posterior probability of the outcome C given the attributes X
  • P(C) is the prior probability of the outcome C
  • P(X|C) is the likelihood which is the probability of attributes given the outcome C
  • P(X) is the prior probability of attributes

Nave Bayes algorithm

  • For a new customer with observed attributes (x1,,xn), the Nave Bayes algorithm computes the probability to be assigned to a class ci given the attributes (x1,,xn), ie P(ci|x1,,xn). By the Bayes theorem, it is given by

\[ \begin{aligned} P(ci|x_1,...,x_n)=\frac{P(x_1,...,x_n|c_i)P(c_i)}{P(x_1,...,x_n)} \end{aligned} \]

  • The Nave Bayes algorithm is based on two simplification assumptions:

    • The conditional independence assumption \[ \begin{aligned} P(x_1,...,x_n|c_i)=\Pi_{j=1}^n P(x_j| c_i) \end{aligned} \]
    • Ignore the denominator P(x1,,xn). Then: \[ \begin{aligned} P(ci|x_1,...,x_n)\propto P(c_i). \Pi_{j=1}^n P(x_j| c_i) \end{aligned} \]

Steps of the Nave Bayes algorithm

  • Step1: From the training set, calculate the probabilities of all class labels, P(ci)

  • Step2: Compute the conditional probabilities of each attribute xj given each class label ci, namely P(xj|ci)

  • Step3: For a new record with observed attributes (x1,,xn), assigns the label ci that maximizes

\[ \begin{aligned} P(c_i). \Pi_{j=1}^n P(x_j| c_i) \end{aligned} \]
